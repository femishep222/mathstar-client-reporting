Summary & Next Steps

+

Imagine this: It's 2034. Jakayah is giving a speech at her college graduation—summa cum laude, full scholarship, heading to grad school. Someone asks how she's always been so brilliant at everything. She pauses. "I wasn't always like this," she says. "In fourth grade, I was in the 'bad kids' class—we were all behind in math, struggling to keep up. But there was this one month when our teachers brought in this weird VR math game. And something just... clicked. I went from being terrified of numbers to finding them fascinating. I didn't see results right away—it took time. But once I stopped being afraid, I could actually hear what Ms. Joseph was teaching. That's when everything started to change."

That's the turning point we're after. That's what MathStar is designed to create.

Here's what we learned at Ellis: MathStar did what it was built to do. Students who normally shut down during math were regulated, engaged, and joyful. Ten out of ten kids chose extra math over recess. Ms. Cruz observed "strong indicators around student regulation, time-on-task, and overall engagement," particularly powerful for EI students who struggle with traditional formats. Both instructors rated the program 10/10.

Here's what we didn't do: We didn't collect the data Ellis needs most—no baseline assessments, no progress monitoring, no systematic evidence of classroom transfer. Ms. Cruz was direct: "Engagement has opened the door...now we need to see what students are learning and how we're monitoring growth over time." She's right, and that's not what MathStar measures.

The reality: Seven of ten students completed the pilot. Ellis sees the value, but expansion isn't viable next year due to budget constraints and a mismatch with your MTSS requirements. We hope we've planted seeds that will grow over time. We're honored to have served your students, and we'd love to partner again if the opportunity arises.
